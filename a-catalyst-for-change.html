A Catalyst for Change

Everything the courts have done since 1954 has been done in the name of enforcing Brown."
          -Raymond Wolters [1]
Brown provided the foundation for further progress in the civil rights movement, raising both the expectations of civil rights groups and the responsibility of the federal government to acknowledge and combat racism. 
The bus-boycott movement in Montgomery arose only one-and-a-half years after Brown. In 1957, President Dwight D. Eisenhower sent in federal troops to Little Rock. This would not have happened without Brown. And in the 1960s, the direct-action phase of the civil rights movement swelled, ultimately forcing Congress to pass the historic Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965."
​            -James T. Patterson [2]
"Several rows of civil rights activists, many holding hands, march down Chicago's Balboa Drive to protest school segregation" (Library of Congress, 1965).
(Leffler, 1963)
Brown’s success in ending legal segregation in education in undeniable. It is appropriately viewed as perhaps the most significant case on race in America’s history. Not only did the Brown opinion lead to more than a dozen unanimous decisions by the Supreme Court finding segregation of public schools unconstitutional or upholding desegregation remedies, it also went a long way toward healing the black communit[y]...”
-Dr. Charles J. Ogletree Jr. [3]
The chronology of events following Brown suggested that it was the first strong link in a chain of causation leading to the great acceleration of the civil rights movement of the 1960s."
           -James T. Patterson [4]
[T]he major value of the Brown decision may have come as a result of well-publicized forms of white resistance that appalled many who otherwise would have remained on the sidelines [of the Civil Rights movement].”
          -Derrick Bell [5]

Symptomatic Treatment for an Institutional Disease

[W]hile Brown effectively spawned the civil rights movement, it also determined in many ways the terms and scope of that movement, which eventually failed to engage the reality of racism directly. The decision in Brown and the remedy of desegregation changed the way in which racism was expressed and discussed in the United States. However, ending legalized segregation and ending discrimination would prove to be two different challenges.”
          -Catherine Prendergast [6]
Brown undoubtedly fueled the fire of the civil rights movement, sparking change in multifarious ways. However, as Brown faces the future, half-solutions hailed as triumphs are giving way to reveal a tragically static level of systematic racism [7].
Short-term gains have been replaced by setbacks engendered by new forms of racism. School districts, briefly integrated, have become resegregated. Some distinctively African-American institutions have been permanently destroyed and others crippled. As we stand near the end or the transformation of affirmative action, things look set to get worse, not better.”
            -Dr. Charles J. Ogletree Jr. [9]
Schools in fact are now more racially segregated than they were at the height of the desegregation effort."
        -Dr. Martha Minow [8]
(Urban Institute, 2012, obtained from Nelson, 2014)
