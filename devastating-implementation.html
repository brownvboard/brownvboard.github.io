Devastating Implementation

The implementation of Brown I through Brown II was deeply flawed, from its inclusion of “all deliberate speed” language which implicitly sanctioned inaction to its failure to direct even the most basic parameters of a remedy, leaving desegregation up to the white local governments and education boards that supported segregation. This ultimately led to a failure to end segregation, the widespread firing of black educators, and the dismantling of black educational institutions. Taken together, this deprived black Americans of opportunities and support in their classroom and communities for decades to come. ​

"All Deliberate Speed": A Euphemism for Endless Delay

'All deliberate speed' has turned out to be only a soft euphemism for delay.”
​                - US Supreme Court Justice Black,   Alexander v. Holmes County Bd. of Ed [1]
"By 1958... seven states—Virginia, South Carolina, Georgia, Alabama, Florida, Mississippi, and Louisiana—maintained public school segregation." - Library of Congress (The Washington Post, 1958)
Brown II, with its decision to proceed with ‘all deliberate speed,’ ensured that the [legal] imperative was not implemented as a social imperative.”
          -Dr. Charles J. Ogletree Jr. [2]
"Because of school boards' reluctance to follow either the letter or the spirit of the law, segregation remained in effect well into the 1960s." - Library of Congress (Library of Congress, The Aftermath of Brown).
The impacts of Brown I were quickly and greatly diminished by Brown II, where the Supreme Court all but ensured that desegregation would remain unenforced.
"Governor George Wallace blocking the entrance to the University of Alabama" - Library of Congress (Leffler, 1963)
The Court relied on 'good faith compliance at the earliest predictable date,' and evident in these words is the haphazard accountability put on states to enforce desegregation and strive for true integration.”
            -Dorinda J, Carter, Stella M. Flores, and Richard J. Reddick [3]
Brown II gave much of the discretion on how to carry out desegregation to to federal district judges ...The district courts were located in communities where ‘the segregated way of life was deemed very close to godliness.’ … As a result, many federal courts in the South delayed desegregation cases for long period and then ordered only limited changes.”
   -Dr. Charles J. Ogletree Jr. [4]
(Dr. Vanessa Walker on “second class integration” C-SPAN, 2018)
​(Dr. Vanessa Walker on worries of black educators, C-SPAN, 2018)

A New Form of Subjugation

Many school districts responded to Brown by firing black school teachers rather than integrating on all levels, destroying a rewarding career path for blacks Americans and an essential support system for black students [5].
Letter of termination to black teacher in 1953 in anticipation of the Brown decision (Tillman, 2004)
(Linda Brown, University of Michigan, 2004, obtained from C-SPAN Media)
(Green, 2004)
...We were under such a terrible strain because when they wanted integration they never considered the effect that it would have on the black teacher who was very qualified. Practically all of them [black teachers] had their masters. . . . As far as the black community, they were very quiet about it but they put such a terrible strain on the black teachers. They didn't care whether the black teachers had jobs or not." 
​            - Mrs. Merrill, Teacher, Washington School, Kansas [6]
(Patton, 1956)
"Telegram from W.C. Patton to organize against a senate bill that would allow Macon County BOE more freedom to fire (black) teachers" - Revisionist History [7]
"The Negro teachers had higher certification levels, and likely more years of experience" (Walker, 1996).
(Washington, 1957)
"Handwritten letter to NAACP from a local member seeking help for his wife, a teacher, who was refused a job" - Revisionist History [8]

"Who's going to tell them they can be anything they want to be?"
                                                                                     -Dr. Vanessa Siddle Walker [9]

Placing black kids in majority white school systems put their destinies in the hands of possibly biased teachers and administrators. 
"Black students indeed are referred to gifted programs, particularly in reading, at significantly lower rates when taught by non-Black teachers, a concerning result given the relatively low incidence of assignment to own-race teachers among Black students" (Grissom and Redding, 2016).
If you look at the honors classes, . . . they’re almost all white. The teachers pick the white kids for those classes because they’re nice and quiet . . . .”
          -Peter H. Irons [10]
“When you look at teacher perceptual data . . . what we see is that teachers are more likely to rate factors like approaches to learning, socio-emotional capabilities, and so forth higher when they race match with those kids, which suggests that what’s possibly going on here is that teachers have different expectations of kids that are correlated with the racial and ethnic match with the teacher and the student, and that might be affecting the referral probability. . . . What’s very likely going on here is the reason that you’re getting this gap is that there’s lots of kids that are never getting the chance to take the test because the teacher never thought there was the potential for giftedness or likelihood of giftedness for that kid.”
                  -Dr. Jason Grissom [11]
The racial-balance goal can be met only in schools where Whites are in the majority and retain control. The quality of schooling Black children receive is determined by what Whites (they of the group who caused the harm in the first place) are willing to provide - which, as we should not be surprised to learn, is not very much.”
              ​-Derrick Bell [12]
("First Day of Desegregation at Ft. Myer Elementary in Ft. Myer, Virginia on September 8, 1954", 1954)
The Portland school board’s policy equated integration and racial assimilation. This policy . . . is a means of socializing nonwhite students to act, speak, and believe very much like white students. It leaves dominant group values intact, does no damage to notions of white superiority, and helps to gain the support of those whites who view it as a means of helping nonwhite peoples to become fully human by instilling them with ‘white’ ways of thinking and feeling.”
         ​-Derrick Bell [13]
Brown led to the dismantling of the very systems that supported the black community in their struggle for equality, destroying the foundations for future success [14].
(Walker, 1999)
[My English teacher] would always tell you wherever you went, always feel that you are important, that you are somebody. And she made every child in the room that way. Because there were some kids that had more than others - their homes were better than others."
          -Nellie Williamson, as quoted in Their Highest Potential [15]
...[In segregated schools,] caring adults gave individual concern, personal time, and so forth to help ensure a learning environment in which African American children would succeed."
          -Dr. Vanessa Siddle Walker [16]
(Dr. Vanessa Walker, Benefits of Education by Black Educators, C-Span, 2018)
